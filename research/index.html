Research

Rulemaking

My dissertation examines the effects of mass political engagement on bureaucratic policymaking. In “Why Do Agencies (Sometimes) Get So Much Mail,” I introduce a dataset of millions of public comments on draft regulations. I find that most public comments are mobilized by a few public interest organizations. A second chapter, “Social Movements, Science, Bureaucracy, and Democracy” focuses on the role of the environmental justice movement in rulemaking through public comments. I find that agencies are more likely to add language addressing environmental justice concerns when such concerns are raised in public comments but no evidence that mass comment campaings make this more likely.

In a separate paper with Susan Webb Yackee, I examine rulemaking across pre- and post-NPRM stages using monthly internal reports from the Department of Transportation and multi-state duration models.

Congressional advocacy and oversight

With Eleanor Neff Powell and Justin Grimmer, I am studying interactions between Members of Congress and other parts of the federal government. Through hundreds of Freedom of Information Act Requests, webscraping, and data wrangling, we have assembled a massive new dataset of letters, emails, and phone calls from Members of Congress to federal agencies. We introduce this dataset in our APSA 2018 paper and are now linking legislator advocacy and oversight behavior to other data such as campaign contributions by energy companies.

In another project on oversight, I use text analysis to study the agenda-setting power of U.S. federal agency budget proposals and appropriations sub-committee reports (see my PolMeth poster on modeling relationships among texts here).

Forestry regulation

Two projects study the role of private actors in regulation through case studies of forestry policy.

“Do Private Regulations `Ratchet Up’” with Constance McDermott and Ben Cashore clarifies debates in the private governance literature by offering methods to measure regulatory stringency. Using these methods, we compare the forest management practices required by the Forest Stewardship Council and Sustainable Forestry Initiative.

A peer-reviewed book chapter, “Non-domestic Sources of Canadian Boreal Forest Policy” with Irene Scher and Ben Cashore assesses the role of U.S. NGOs in the Canada’s boreal forest policy.
